# Competitor research — pet social & walk apps

Compiled to inform the boop investor brief. Focus: **how competitors monetize, and where their users revolt.**

The thesis we're building: *competitors monetize the walk directly (in-app currency, credits, subscription gates) and have to devalue the economy the moment they scale. Boop monetizes the outfit on the avatar — engagement and commerce are decoupled, so we never have to tax our most active users.*

| Android | Available, install count not retrieved | Play Store |

### Model
Walk-to-earn. Users earn "bones" (in-app currency) by logging walks, maintaining streaks, referring friends, verifying locations, voting in polls, and checking in. Bones redeem in an in-app marketplace integrated with Shopify, can cover up to 100% of order value.

### The "reset mechanism" — buried in the FAQ
Direct quote from levmaps.com/faqs: *"if your Bones balance remains positive for 30 consecutive days, the balance will reset to zero."*

This is the policy that triggered the early-2025 backlash, expressed in the company's own words. It is structurally hostile to saving: holding bones zeroes you out, so users are forced to spend within 30 days or lose everything. Combined with the 80% value cut documented in user reviews, the math is brutal — accumulated balances were both shrunk *and* time-bombed in a single update.

The FAQ also reserves the right to adjust earning and redemption rates "to prevent excessive accumulation" — i.e. the company has formally documented that the most engaged users (the ones with the largest balances) are the cost center.

### What users loved
- Walk tracking + photo/video sharing (5★ reviews mention "fun way to connect")
- Local meetups → real-world friendships
- Real, redeemable rewards (when bones held value)

### What broke the trust (early 2025)
- **Bone value cut 80%** ($0.50 → $0.10) without user notice
- **30-day expiration** added to previously perpetual currency
- Glitches (login issues, broken streaks) compounded the perception of bad faith

### Sample review excerpts
- *"Disappointing changes and glitches"* — bone value drop + expiration + glitches; user considered deletion despite previously loving the app
- *"Disappointed"* — initial love → discouragement after simultaneous expiration + 80% devaluation
- *"Lev bone rate"* — users blindsided on first redemption attempt

### Developer response pattern
Defensive ("earning methods haven't changed", citing "growth-related rebalancing"). Reads as not hearing the actual complaint: perceived value collapse + sunk-cost loss of accumulated bones.

### Strategic takeaway for boop
**Walk-to-earn works for acquisition, fails as monetization.** Once the economy needs rebalancing, your most loyal users churn first because they hold the most currency. The model is structurally fragile because it conflates the engagement reward with the spend currency.

Boop's wedge: monetize the *outfit* (a discrete commerce decision), not the *walk* (an engagement loop). The walk drives engagement; the outfit drives revenue. They never compete.

## Sniffspot — Private Dog Parks

**Model:** Marketplace for renting private yards as dog parks. Hosts list yards, users book by the hour.

### What users loved
- 95% of visits get 5★
- Quality of host experiences (water, toys, privacy)
- Dogs visibly happier post-visit

### What broke the trust
- **Switched to subscription model** ($20/mo) with **expiring credits**
- "Was great until subscription model" is a recurring review opener
- Casual users (don't visit weekly) priced out — value proposition collapses for the long tail
- Trust incidents: fraudulent listings, hosts requesting extra deposits, one case of a host who didn't actually own the property (trespassing risk)

### Strategic takeaway for boop
**Second confirmation of the same pattern.** Sniffspot ran a transactional marketplace, then bolted a subscription wall on top to fix unit economics — and lost the casual majority. Same lesson as Lev: monetization that taxes the core behavior alienates the users who validated the product.

The pattern is now a **thesis**, not an anecdote: *pet apps that monetize the engagement loop directly have to devalue it to scale, and lose their base when they do.*

**Hardware-led recurring revenue.** App is free; revenue comes from collar hardware (~$150) and recurring membership ($19/mo per published material). The app exists primarily to make the collar useful — not as a standalone product.

### Strategic takeaway for boop
**Different category, different lesson.** Fi is the dominant pet hardware app and proves recurring pet revenue at scale (35K ratings is ~1M+ install band). But its model is gated by hardware adoption — slow, expensive, B2C-hardware unit economics.

Boop's relevant takeaway: Fi's user base validates that pet parents *will* pay recurring fees for a pet-specific app. We just don't have to make them buy a $150 collar to enter the funnel.

- Positive: "It's nice to see people posting cute videos instead of politics all the time" — confirms boop's "saner feed" thesis

## Sniffspot — Private Dog Parks (full)

### Vitals

| Field | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Full name | Sniffspot - Private Dog Parks | App Store |
| Developer | Sniffspot, Inc. | App Store |
| iOS rating | 4.9 ★ / ~20,000 US ratings | App Store |
| iOS price | Free; subscription via web ($20/mo, web-side checkout) | App Store + web |
| iOS age rating | 4+ | App Store |
| iOS category | Lifestyle | App Store |
| Latest version | 2.20.1, updated within days of fetch | App Store |

### Model
Marketplace for renting private yards. Originally per-visit booking; transitioned to subscription wall ($20/mo + expiring credits).

### Backlash
Most-recent reviews dominated by subscription complaints. Casual users priced out. Trust incidents (unauthorized listings, host fraud) compound the issue.

### Notable
**App Store IAP shows none** — the subscription appears to be sold via web checkout, likely to avoid Apple's 30% cut. This is a tell: when monetization is hidden from the App Store IAP listing, it's almost always because the unit economics can't survive Apple's tax. Worth flagging for investors as a competitor-fragility signal.

### Strategic takeaway for boop
Second confirmed instance of *monetize-the-engagement-loop → backlash* pattern. Sniffspot has 20K ratings (mid-six-figure user base) and still couldn't make booking-only economics work — they had to add a subscription, and the most loyal segment churned. The pattern is now thesis-grade.

### Patterns visible

1. **Two of two apps that monetized engagement directly hit user revolts** (Lev bones, Sniffspot credits). 100% of the sample. Small sample, but consistent direction.
2. **Free social-only apps stall at low ratings** (Pawmates 98, Social Paws 5, DOGOUT <5). No monetization → no incentive to scale, no flywheel.
3. **DogHood's $99.99 Premium price** is the highest validated subscription price in the cohort. Suggests boop's $4.99 target may be conservative.
4. **Sniffspot avoids Apple's 30%** by selling subscription via web — a tell that unit economics are tight in pet marketplace categories.
5. **Hardware (Fi) has the largest install base by far** (35K ratings). Hardware moat is real but slow; software-first plays must compensate with social mechanics or commerce velocity.

## How to use this in the investor brief

Three places to deploy this research:

1. **Why now / problem section** — "Every existing pet app that has tried to monetize directly has lost its base." Cite Lev + Sniffspot.
2. **Business model section** — defend boop's model by contrasting: "We don't monetize the walk. The walk is free forever. We monetize the outfit."
3. **Competition slide** — add a column: *"Monetization durable to scale?"* Boop = ✓, all others = ✗ or unproven.
